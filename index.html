Towards Instructing Disease-Drug Link Prediction with Social Determinants of Health

Authors: Yashaswi Galhotra*, Ying Ding, Li Shen, Huanmei Wu, Tianlong Chen, Kaixiong Zhou

Conference: ACM-BCB, 2024

Abstract:

In this study, we emphasize the importance of incorporating Social Determinants of Health (SDoH)—such as economic, educational, environmental, and behavioral factors—into per- sonalized medicine. These factors are often overlooked by re- searchers due to challenges like fragmented knowledge, non- standardized research repositories, and evolving ontologies. Excluding SDoH factors limits the accuracy and effectiveness of disease-drug interaction predictions, which are essential for precision medicine. To overcome these challenges, we introduced the BioSocialNet framework, which integrates SDoH factors into knowledge graph construction and uses Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) for more accurate predictions. BioSocialNet consists of two key components: (1) construction of a comprehensive knowledge graph that merges biomedical and SDoH data, and (2) deployment of GCNs that model the complex interactions between these factors. We applied this framework to incorporate SDoH data from the MIMIC III dataset into PrimeKG, a comprehen- sive knowledge graph that includes a wide range of diseases, drugs, and biomedical interactions. We then trained a GCN model on this enriched graph, resulting in a significant im- provement in disease-drug linkage prediction accuracy, from 88.05% to 94.08%. This finding underscores the pivotal role of SDoH in enhancing the precision and implications of person- alized medicine, providing a clear pathway for more accurate and equitable healthcare interventions.

Three-way classification of Infant Fussing and Crying in Real-world Environments

Authors: Yashaswi Galhotra*, Priyanka Khante, Kaya de Barbaro

Submitted to: ICASSP, 2025

Preview:

There has been a proliferation of models for automated infant cry detection. With the publication of novel real-world datasets of infant distress, recent models have begun to generalize beyond controlled environments. However, most cry detection models are trained in controlled environments, meaning they are not likely to generalize to real-world settings. Additionally, most models do not differentiate between levels of distress, i.e. between fussing versus crying, which is essential for determining clinical appropriateness of response. In this paper, we present a machine learning approach leveraging deep features to classify infant distress in natural environments. Our two-way model (distress vs. non-distress) achieved an F1 score of 0.76 (Precision: 0.72, Recall: 0.84), demonstrating significantly improved external validity over prior real-world models. Our three-way model (cry vs. fuss vs. non-distress) achieved an F1 score of 0.59 (Precision: 0.55, Recall: 0.67), providing a new baseline for real-world classification. We release a comprehensive dataset of annotated real-world infant fuss and cry vocalizations to support further research in this area.

Leveraging AI to Automatically Parse Parent-child Activities and Affect from Longform Audio Recordings

SRCD, 2025

Summary:

There is growing recognition of the importance of capturing children’s everyday experiences as critical "inputs" to development (de Barbaro & Fausey, 2022). Developmental scientists are increasingly using wearable sensors, such as the Language ENvironment Analysis (LENA), a lightweight audio recorder worn by children, to capture up to 24 hours of continuous audio in their everyday home environments. While this provides unparalleled access to children’s everyday experiences, it also presents a significant challenge: how to efficiently analyze large volumes of audio data to extract meaningful activity from recordings. In the current work, our goal is to leverage and test the efficacy of existing AI tools to parse real-world child-worn audio recordings to identify 1) everyday household activities, including including meals, playing, bedtime, reading, etc, and 2) emotional and mental states of family members, including joy, frustration feelings of anxiety, and tantrums. Our aim is to develop a pipeline to provide developmental scientists an entry point to identify areas of interest for more detailed human annotation.
